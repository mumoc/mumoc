## ABOUT SECTION

I help teams move forward by building the conditions where they can do it themselves.

For 15+ years I've worked across every layer of software organizations: engineering, design, QA, product, operations, eCommerce, and people leadership. I've held roles from Software Engineer to COO, moving between them deliberately — not chasing titles, but learning how organizations function as systems.

I am a Team Architect and a Connector. I absorb ambiguity, bridge people and systems, and translate unclear business needs into actionable technical paths. Even when my title is Senior Software Engineer, I naturally operate beyond that boundary — because that's how I work, not what the role requires.

The thing I'm most proud of isn't a product. It's the conditions I've built for others.

As COO, I helped take an organization from negative NPS to 99, and eNPS from 10 to 95 in six months. Not through metrics and mandates, but through care, trust, and building systems that let people grow. Then the organization asked me to stop prioritizing people and start treating them as cost lines. I chose integrity over advancement and returned to engineering. Not a step back. A realignment with my principles.

My framework is Care to Deliver: Care → Trust → Teamwork → Delivery. Most organizations try to deliver first and care later. I reverse that. When teams share openly and care about each other, trust grows and collaboration becomes natural. This isn't soft — it directly produced those NPS numbers. The human conditions and the business outcomes are the same thing.

On AI: I treat it as a cognitive collaborator, not a productivity tool. Most powerful when integrated at the team level — onboarded with the same progressive trust you'd give any new teammate. What concerns me is organizations using AI to scale activity when the real opportunity is improving judgment, direction, and accountability. AI amplifies what already works. If the culture is broken, AI makes the noise louder.

I'm looking for a fertile field — a company early enough in its growth that I can help build the right foundations before wrong habits take root. I write about all of this at medium.com/@mumoc.

Based in Colima, Mexico. Open to conversations about team dynamics, technical leadership, and AI integration.

Post A: The Move Back to Engineering

The organization I was COO of asked me to stop prioritizing people.

Start treating them as cost lines. Notify some of them late — late enough that they couldn't respond.

I'd taken the company from negative NPS to 99. From eNPS of 10 to 95 in six months. Not by optimizing metrics. By actually caring.

And now I was being asked to use that same trust against the people who had it in me.

I left.

Not in anger. I spent time reflecting, wondering if I was the problem. Then I understood: staying would require me to become someone I wasn't willing to be.

I went back to engineering. It wasn't a step down. It was a realignment.

If your title and your values are pulling in different directions — that discomfort is information. The right move is the one that lets you stay yourself.

---

Post B: AI Joins Teams, Not Individuals

Most companies are introducing AI the same way.

Give everyone access. Wait for productivity to spike.

But something else is happening first.

Someone admits they don't know how the tool works. Someone else helps. A conversation starts. Two people from different teams who never spoke are suddenly talking about what they're building, what's hard, and yes — sometimes about games and hobbies.

That moment of shared vulnerability? That's where trust begins.

AI didn't isolate us. It gave us a reason to ask for help.

The real question isn't "how do we give AI access to everyone?"

It's: how do we introduce AI as a new member of the team?

---

Post C: The Hiring Mistake Nobody Talks About

Most hiring rubrics are evaluated against an abstract ideal.

Does this person know Ruby? 10 = yes, 0 = no.

That's not how teams work.

The question isn't whether someone is good. It's whether they're good for this team, right now, at this stage.

In the hiring methodology I built, 5 on a 1 to 10 scale means your team's current average. Not the world's.

Above 5: this person will raise your standard.
Below 5: this person will lower it.

The goal isn't to hire impressive people. It's to always improve the team you have.

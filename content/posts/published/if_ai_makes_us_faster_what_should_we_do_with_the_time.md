# If AI Makes Us Faster, What Should We Do With the Time?

I know everyone is talking about AI.

AI here. AI there. It matters — of course it does.
I’ve been part of those conversations too.

But often, the discussion quickly shifts to productivity. And it sounds like this:

> “I created a ticket and AI implemented it.”<br/>
> “Now we can deliver more tickets.”<br/>
> “If we deliver more tickets, how much cost can we reduce?”<br/>
> “Do we really need all those people?”<br/>

Yeah. Speed is useful. Efficiency matters.

But the deeper I get into integrating AI into real workflows, the more I feel we’re asking the wrong question.

The question is not:

**“How can we do more work?”**

A better question is:

**“What kind of work deserves to be done?"**

## Thinking Is Becoming Expensive as Execution Gets Cheaper

AI makes output cheap.

- Code can be generated
- Plans can be drafted
- Reports can be assembled
- Backlogs can be filled

Execution, once a constraint, becomes cheap. Almost trivial.

And when something becomes cheaper, its value changes.

Humans can’t compete with machines on speed.

So we must focus on what machines can’t meaningfully own:

Not more activity. **Better judgment.**

## Productivity Is Moving Upstream

For years, productivity lived downstream:

- Shipping features
- Closing tasks
- Delivering artifacts
- Completing milestones

But AI now handles much of that execution layer.

So human productivity shifts *upstream*:

- Choosing the right problems
- **Framing challenges correctly**
- Defining success clearly
- Understanding constraints
- Evaluating tradeoffs

If the problem is wrong, faster execution just gets you to the wrong place sooner.

> Does this resonate?<br/>
> I’ve lost count of how many AI “shortcuts” turned into cleanup work because the problem wasn’t clear.

## The Most Valuable Work Is Often Invisible

Real value rarely appears in metrics:

- Saying “Hey — this is not the right problem”
- Removing unnecessary complexity
- Clarifying ambiguous goals
- Anticipating second-order effects
- Connecting decisions across teams

These actions don’t produce artifacts. They produce coherence.

And coherence keeps systems from collapsing under their own weight.

AI can accelerate production. But it cannot ensure coherence.

That’s still human work.

## Using AI Well Often Requires Slowing Down

There’s a paradox in AI adoption.

Tools make execution faster.

But using them well requires more deliberate thinking.

You must:

- Check assumptions
- Validate outputs
- Understand limitations
- Anticipate misuse
- Recognize when automation hides risk

Speed without reflection creates fragile systems.

Thoughtful pacing creates resilient ones.

>_A game is only late until it ships. But it’s bad forever._

## AI Alone Doesn’t Improve Productivity

Here’s what many AI conversations quietly ignore:

AI-driven productivity gains tend to appear **only when organizations redesign how they work**.

- Not just processes
- Not just tools
- But structures
- And **culture**

If incentives stay the same…
If metrics still reward activity over outcomes…
If visibility matters more than impact…

Faster tools simply amplify old mistakes.

AI is a multiplier.

- If systems prioritize **speed** → we get faster **noise**
- If systems prioritize **clarity** → we get amplified **insight**
- If systems prioritize **output** → we drown in **artifacts**
- If systems prioritize **understanding** → we build **leverage**

AI doesn’t decide what matters.

**WE do.**

The difference isn’t technical.

It’s structural. And deeply cultural.

Because productivity isn’t just about how work flows —

it’s about how organizations define what *counts* as valuable work.

## A Powerful Shift

Before AI, being productive meant being efficient with effort.

After AI, being productive means being intentional with attention.

Effort scales.

Attention doesn’t.

And attention determines direction.

If AI gives us more capacity, the goal isn’t filling it with more tasks.

The opportunity is investing that capacity in:

- Deeper problem exploration
- Cross-team alignment
- System simplification
- Strategic thinking
- Long-term design

Work that was always important —
but rarely urgent enough to prioritize.


> You remember those ideas that were pitched but never saw the light, right?<br/>
> Phase 2. Phase 3. Phase X.<br/>
> All buried under “urgent” requests and impossible delivery promises.


## Choosing A Better Question

The next time productivity comes up in an AI discussion, ask:

- Were these the right things to pursue?
- Did we reduce future complexity?
- Did we make better decisions than before?
- Did we leave the system clearer?

Less about volume. More about selection.

Productivity becomes strategic — not mechanical.

Pause ...

and ask:

> “What kind of work becomes more valuable when speed is no longer scarce?”

That’s where human contribution evolves.

That’s where real productivity lives.

---

AI reduces the cost of execution.

So the value of thoughtful direction increases.

And maybe productivity, in the age of AI, is no longer about doing more work —

but about making sure the work we choose truly matters.

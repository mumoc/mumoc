# Execution is becoming cheap

Not long ago, execution was the constraint.

You needed whole teams from different disciplines just to ship something.

- Engineerzs to "write code"
- Managers to "coordinate"
- Product Owners to "guide"
- QA to "ensure quality"
- Designers to "build experiences"

Remember all the discussions about Waterfall, or Agile, or Kanban...
MVPs, quick prototypes, product design sprints...

Everyone trying to keep costs as low as possible.
Everyone trying to find the new idea — least effort, most value.

Now it's not anymore.

AI can execute.

Faster. Cheaper. At scale. On every area. No distinction.

> "Oh, we focus on the product, focus on validation,
> let's interview people, focus on the customer experience —
> then, maybe then, we can think about planning..."
>
> Just an example of one of many scenarios that used to happen. I heard it a lot of times.

---

## The struggle today

I'm seeing many teams and individuals struggling with this.

They are *"optimized"* for execution, for doing.

**Not for thinking**.

I'm seeing people not even trying.

Just "working" to "deliver what they've been told."

Or just doing things to still feel important.

Not even caring when everyone around them is saying *"hey, this is not good."*

Using their authority to push "AI" — without understanding what that even means.

> Is it just me?
> 
> In my experience, I'm seeing managers handling AI with no real understanding of their own work —
> giving vague instructions like: "do it fine. Implement best practices."
>
> Calling themselves "experts" and saying "we can remove a lot of people with my workflows."
>
> Is this more common than I think?

---

## What remains valuable

But if execution became cheaper, does that mean all those people are irrelevant?

No.

It's just that we need to understand the real value, again:

- Direction.
- Judgment.
- Context.

Knowing **what** is relevant to BUILD.
Knowing **why** it matters.
Knowing **when** to move, to do it.

Execution is still needed.

But it's no longer the differentiator.

Now, more than ever, the years of experience you have — or the amount of knowledge you think you have — is becoming less and less relevant on its own.

It's more about the skills you've built: the ability to think, to plan, to understand as an individual.
And also to lead, to evaluate, to guide your team, to collaborate.

With context. With judgment. With direction.

> Like it always has been.
>
> It was never about the AMOUNT of knowledge.
> It was always about the ability to connect everyone and everything with logic.

**THINKING** is the work.

And if you don't adapt to that shift… well.

**The differentiator has moved. Most people still haven't noticed.**

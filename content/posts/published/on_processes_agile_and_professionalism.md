# On Processes, "Agile" and Professionalism

## Setting the Scene

Suppose...

- You meet a client with a fantastic idea — THE business — and they want to launch it quickly and with great quality: AN MVP.
- You decide to start IMMEDIATELY.
- You begin by creating THE BEST brand, selling the pitch you like most.
- You design an EXCELLENT user experience and interface without understanding the technical, operational, or business complications.
- You coordinate and implement while assuming answers to many unknowns, without thorough and quality follow-up.
- In the end, the client discovers it's incredibly difficult to use and far from finished.

## Enter the 3 R's

They decide to Re-define, Re-organize, and Re-implement.

- Explaining the operational problems and issues with the presented design.
- Analyzing the complexity of the various features.
- Aligning business needs and requirements.
- Limiting scope under the understanding of an MVP.
- Asking questions and organizing constant reviews.

All with the joint participation of your team and the client's.

In short: Teamwork and Effective Communication.

## But What's Wrong with This?

Impositions, assumptions, disconnects, lack of ownership/professionalism, lack of coordination, attention to detail, and participation.

These make an MVP stretch on for months and months, when it could have been built collaboratively and in an organized way, speeding up delivery.

## Recommendations

### Theoretical

Remember the Agile Manifesto:
  - Individuals and interactions over processes and tools
  - Working software over comprehensive documentation
  - Customer collaboration over contract negotiation
  - Responding to change over following a plan

Client Profiling:
  - Every client is different in how they work and their level of experience
  - Profiling (think UX PERSONA) helps understand how to bring them into our processes
  - How we'll get involved to achieve objectives

Professionalism (Ownership):
  - Internal Product Owners / We are all owners
  - Being a PO doesn't mean you hold the truth; it makes you responsible for understanding the business and aligning the effort

Follow best practices:
  - Code, Engineering, Design, Marketing/Branding, QA, Management, etc.

Use the Client / Vendor Framework:
  - Every actor in the process is a CLIENT or VENDOR at some point

Teamwork, Growth Mindset:
  - Team instead of a group of individuals
  - Leverage the collective knowledge cloud

### Practical

All things SCRUM and similar:
  - At least one weekly sync with:
    - Key Results
    - Next Objectives
    - Blockers and Questions

Project Briefing:
  - Define purpose (why) and objective (what)
  - Business goals
  - Target market
  - Success metrics
  - Requirements

Planning (roadmap):
  - Organize and prioritize stories
  - Define assumptions
  - Scope

  > Plans are useless, but planning is everything
  >
  > -- Dwight Eisenhower

Source of Truth:
  - Board as the source of truth for the project
  - Cards as the source of truth for the story

Quality Standards (LEAN):
  - Acceptance criteria defined for each story

Disposable MVPs:
  - An MVP by nature will be disposable in the future

Don't reinvent the wheel.

## So Where Do Processes, Agile, and Professionalism Stand?

The dumbest thing we can do is fit everything into the same framework.

YES, we must have a defined process (journey map).
YES, we must have quality standards.
YES, we must be strict about following processes.

BUT...

We need to know when to bend and even break the rules.

Today we do our best based on our experience and knowledge — and this may not be equally effective tomorrow, but it will surely be a good guide.
